1.1

The Root Cause

While freelancers responded citing many different problems, in mapping them out we noticed that the majority are second and third order effects from a single source: little or limited knowledge on how to freelance. Unsurprisingly, 61% told us they learnt through "trial and error".

1.2

A Precarious Position

Freelancers who struggle to find good work, may feel compelled to take on low-paying projects leading to a negative feedback loop, further compounding their struggles. Inconsistent or low-paying work is the single biggest reason for freelancer's to return to full-time employment.

Another Approach

Well designed systems can also protect us from our own worst behaviors. Inverting our perspective, we asked:

"Rather than educating freelancer's to help improve their work prospects, why don't we design a system that improves their work prospects and educates them in the process?"

The two primary ways freelancer's find work online is through job boards (formally, or informally like subreddits, Facebook groups, and Twitter) or via marketplaces like Upwork, Fiverr & Freelancer.com.

Only the marketplace model can facilitate the entire flow - from job discovery, to acquisition, delivery & payment while incorporating important mechanisms sought by employers to establish trust, reliability & resolve disputes.

1.4

Rational Employers, Irrational Freelancers

42% of employers told us they had issues with freelancers disappearing mid-project. Reliability was one of several issues cited by employers who like the cost-effectiveness and flexibility of hiring freelancers, but are wary of the "unknown element" inherent in each freelancer's idiosyncratic workflow and way of doing business.

Employers want to know:

1

Can they do the work?

2

Are they reliable?

3

Are they consistent?

4

Are they good communicators?

5

Are they well organized?

Current marketplaces are not perfect, but are valued for simplyfing the process of finding, assessing and hiring the right freelancer for their project, and the protection they extend in the event of a negative or unsatisfactory outcome.

1.6

A great way to find bad jobs

While employers' experiences on these platforms were mixed, freelancers' were almost universally bad: economics subverts the user experience.

1

2

3

4

Without an artificial price floor.

Forcing high competition for low paying projects.

Reputation The skewed supply & demand also corrupts a platforms reputation system. With so many freelancers chasing so few jobs, employers can easily filter out freelancers without a perfect reputation and still find plenty of candidates for their project. Conversely, an employer's negative rating will be overlooked by a freelancer desperate for work.

A negative power dynamic can emerge, with an employer holding the threat of a poor rating against a freelancer to extract more work or unfair conditions during a project.

Power Laws & The Pareto Principle The current flawed reputation model for online marketplaces leads to power laws that entrench the top 20% while discouraging the long tail of new and casual freelancers from establishing themselves.

Incentivization Finally, because the employers are the ones with the money, the platform is incentivized to favor them in any dispute. While anecdotal, numerous freelancers we spoke to felt they experienced this (alongside others who have published their experiences ).

Favoring Friction

These marketplaces were close to perfect, which made them lousy places to find good work. Ironically, adding friction could improve things by intelligently restricting the distribution of jobs to:

1

Reduce competition per job

2

Improve matching for freelancers & employers

3

Give employers new metrics to guide hiring

In the end, employers don't want to be inundated with choice. They just want to be given the right one.
