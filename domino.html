Research

1.0

Starting with the problem

As former freelancers, we had first-hand experience with the common problems that freelancers face. We wanted to make the experience of freelancing easier and simpler.

Finding work Getting paid Time management Pricing Socializing Self-Promotion Sales & Negotiations Confidence Cashflow management Communication Skills Confidence

We began to research the details within these problems so we could move beyond our personal anecdotes.

1.1

The Root Cause

While freelancers responded citing many different problems, in mapping them out we noticed that the majority are second and third order effects from a single source: little or limited knowledge on how to freelance. Unsurprisingly, 61% told us they learnt through "trial and error".

1.2

No Work = No Play

Lacking the knowledge on how to find and win freelance jobs leads to limited, low-paying, or no work which can create a negative feedback loop, further compounding a freelancer's misery.

1.3

Fish v Fishing

While the root cause had been identified, the solution wasn’t to write a book. We learn most effectively through personal experiences, and we’re suckers for incentives.

Well designed systems can also protect us from our own worst behaviors. Inverting our perspective, we asked:

"Rather than educating freelancer's to help improve their work prospects, why don't we design a system that improves their work prospects and educates them in the process?"

The two primary ways freelancer's find work online is through job boards (formally, or informally like subreddits, Facebook groups, and Twitter) or via marketplaces like Upwork, Fiverr & Freelancer.com.

Only the marketplace model can facilitate the entire flow - from job discovery, to acquisition, delivery & payment while incorporating important mechanisms to establish trust, reliability & dispute resolution.

1.4

The map is not the territory

Source

To better understand these marketplaces, we began creating maps of their user flows for both buyers and sellers. How did their account sign-up process work? How quickly did it take to create a profile? Where were the points of friction? And how easy was it to find and hire freelancer's we could trust?

Simultaneously, we began researching freelancer's and employers experiences on these platforms.

1.5

A great way to find bad jobs

While employers' experiences on these platforms were mixed, freelancers' were almost universally bad: economics subverts the user experience.

1

A global labor pool skews supply.

2

Favoring freelancers with lower costs of living.

3

And depresses wages.

4

Without an artificial price floor.

5

Forcing high competition for low paying projects.

Reputation The skewed supply & demand also corrupts a platforms reputation system. With so many freelancers chasing so few jobs, the poor reputation of an individual employer may be considered an acceptable risk of doing business.

Conversly, an employer can hold excessive power over any freelancer they hire through threatening a poor rating. Freelancers below a minimum reputation threshold can be filtered out by employers, without limiting their ability to find someone for their project.

Power Laws & The Pareto Principle Many marketplaces publicly promote the number of projects a freelancer has completed - an artificial (and IMO inaccurate) metric that attempts to convey experience, quality and reliability, but ultimately just tells you how much time a freelancer has put into the platform in question. With so much competition, a rational employer will rely on the few data points they have on hand when assessing the best person for a job.

This leads to a power law that disproportionately favors freelancers with high reputations that have completed a lot of projects, leading to a long tail of disappointment for many, many others.

Incentivization Finally, because the employers are the ones with the money, the platform is incentivized to favor them in any dispute. While anecdotal, numerous freelancers we spoke to felt they experienced this (alongside others who have published their experiences ).

Favoring Friction

These marketplaces were close to perfect, which made them lousy places to find good work. Ironically, adding friction could improve things by intelligently restricting the distribution of jobs to:

1

Reduce competition per job

2

Improve matching between freelancers & jobs

3

Give employers better metrics to guide hiring

In the end, employers don't want to be inundated with choice. They just want to be given the right one.
