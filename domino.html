Inverting our perspective, we asked:

"Rather than educating freelancer's to help improve their work prospects, why don't we design a system that improves their work prospects and educates them in the process?"

The two primary ways freelancer's find work online is through job boards (formally, or informally like subreddits, Facebook groups, and Twitter) or via marketplaces like Upwork, Fiverr & Freelancer.com.

Only the marketplace model can facilitate the entire flow - from job discovery, to acquisition, delivery & payment while incorporating important mechanisms sought by employers to establish trust, reliability & resolve disputes.

Rational Employers, Irrational Freelancers

42% of employers told us they had issues with freelancers disappearing mid-project. Reliability was one of several issues cited by employers who like the cost-effectiveness and flexibility of hiring freelancers, but are wary of the "unknown element" inherent in each freelancer's idiosyncratic workflow and way of doing business.

Current marketplaces aren't perfect, but are valued by employers for simplyfing the process of finding, assessing and hiring the right freelancer for their project, alongside the protection they receive in the event of a negative or unsatisfactory outcome.

5

Forcing high competition for low paying projects.

Reputation The skewed supply & demand also corrupts a platforms reputation system. With so many freelancers chasing so few jobs, employers can easily filter out freelancers without a perfect reputation and still find plenty of candidates for their project. Conversely, an employer's negative rating will be overlooked by a freelancer desperate for work.

A negative power dynamic can emerge, with an employer holding the threat of a poor rating against a freelancer to extract more work or unfair conditions during a project.

Power Laws & The Pareto Principle The flawed reputation model used by online marketplaces leads to power laws that entrench the top 20% while discouraging the long tail of new and casual freelancers from establishing themselves.

Incentivization Finally, because the employers are the ones with the money, the platform is incentivized to favor them in any dispute. While anecdotal, numerous freelancers we spoke to felt they experienced this (alongside plenty of stories elsewhere ).

The Core Interaction

Every marketplace and platform is predicated on a set of fundamental actions: their core interaction.

Domino's core interaction was recommending people you trust to do a great job. The basis of our marketplace's job distribution mechanism.

A marketplace has a lot of moving parts: search, profiles, a reputation system, communication, transaction processing, payouts, support and dispute resolution. Broadly, these functions fall into one of two categories:

We were a bootstrapped team of two and needed to pick our battles. We had initially set out to build the marketplace in its entirety, before backtracking to focus our efforts.

We sought to build a smaller piece of the marketplace, gain traction, then build the remaining components sequentially.

3.2

Distribution

Distribution is a complex problem in any business.

We were encouraged that 77% of freelancers told us they had sent and received referrals to and from their trusted friends, however the distribution of these jobs was hampered by a few factors:

1

It was a slow, manual process mostly via email

2

The jobs were time-sensitive

3

Referrals were often only to groups of 4-6 people

4

The referrals were untracked phantom data

Could we make this existing behavior simpler, faster and easier? Could we track it? Would it increase how far these jobs travel, and improve the success average of matches between employers & freelancers?
